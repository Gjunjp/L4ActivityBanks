Level 4 Activity Banks

For CEFR B2 Level | 20–30 minutes | Click a card to read

Vocabulary Games

Master idioms, phrasal verbs, collocations, and register shifts.

1

Idiom Splat

Goal: Identify figurative language.

Students race to swat the correct idiom or phrasal verb based on contextual scenarios.

Read more
2 clean flyswatters, whiteboard markers.
  1. Write 15 B2-level idioms or phrasal verbs on the whiteboard (e.g., "put up with", "under the weather").
  2. Divide the class into two teams. One representative from each team comes to the front.
  3. Read a detailed scenario (e.g., "I've been sneezing all day and have a terrible headache. I'm feeling...").
  4. The first student to swat the correct expression wins a point.
Instead of just giving definitions, always use vivid scenarios. This forces students to process the pragmatic function of the idiom rather than just its literal translation.
2

Advanced Taboo

Goal: Explain abstract concepts.

Students explain abstract nouns or complex verbs without using the most obvious related terms.

Read more
Cards featuring abstract target words (e.g., Globalization, Procrastinate) and 3 forbidden words.
  1. A student draws a card with an abstract B2 word (e.g., "Sustainability").
  2. They must describe the concept to their team without using the 3 forbidden words (e.g., "Green", "Environment", "Future").
  3. The team has one minute to guess. If they guess correctly, they earn a point.
This demands high-level paraphrasing skills. Encourage students to use relative clauses and hypothetical situations ("Imagine a world where...") to bypass the forbidden words.
3

Nuance Bingo

Goal: Distinguish gradable/ungradable adjectives.

Students mark their boards by identifying extreme adjectives or specific nuanced synonyms.

Read more
Blank paper, pens, a list of extreme/nuanced vocabulary.
  1. Write 16 strong adjectives on the board (e.g., devastated, ecstatic, terrified, spotless).
  2. Students draw a 3x3 grid and fill it with 9 words.
  3. Call out base adjectives (e.g., "Very sad" or "Very clean").
  4. Students must cross out the corresponding extreme adjective. The first to get a line shouts "Bingo!".
To further challenge B2 learners, review the rules of adverb collocations before the game (e.g., reminding them we say "absolutely devastated", not "very devastated").
4

Collocation Dominoes

Goal: Build strong lexical chunks.

Students connect verb-noun or adverb-adjective pairs to create a long chain of accurate collocations.

Read more
Sets of domino cards printed with half-collocations (e.g., "highly" on the right, "make" on the left).
  1. Divide students into small groups and deal out 7 domino cards to each student.
  2. Place one starter card in the center.
  3. Students take turns adding a card to either end, ensuring it forms a natural B2 collocation (e.g., matching "highly" with "recommended", or "make" with "an effort").
  4. They must use the newly formed collocation in a complex sentence to validate their move.
If a group gets stuck, allow them to consult a collocation dictionary. At the B2 level, learning *how* to verify word combinations is as important as memorizing them.
5

Register Shift

Goal: Adapt tone and formality.

Teams race to rewrite a highly informal text message into a formal, academic, or professional email.

Read more
A printed text message full of slang and informal phrasal verbs.
  1. Provide groups with an informal text (e.g., "Hey, sorry I can't make it to the meeting. I'm completely snowed under with work. Let's figure something out later.").
  2. Groups must rewrite the text into a highly formal email to a CEO or professor.
  3. They must swap phrasal verbs for Latinate verbs (e.g., "make it" -> "attend", "snowed under" -> "occupied", "figure out" -> "arrange").
  4. Groups present their upgraded texts for class evaluation.
This is crucial for exam writing sections. Discuss why certain words sound more formal (often due to their Latin/French origins vs. Germanic phrasal roots).
6

The Interrogation

Goal: Use target words defensively.

A "suspect" must seamlessly weave a list of random B2 vocabulary words into their alibi while being questioned.

Read more
A secret list of 5 unrelated B2 vocabulary words.
  1. One student (the suspect) comes to the front and receives a secret list of 5 difficult words (e.g., mandatory, skeptical, crucial, out of the blue, consequence).
  2. The class acts as detectives, aggressively questioning the suspect about their whereabouts last night.
  3. The suspect must answer the questions naturally while secretly forcing all 5 words into their narrative.
  4. At the end, the detectives must guess which 5 words were the target vocabulary.
This tests cognitive agility. If the suspect uses the word too unnaturally or pauses awkwardly, the "detectives" will easily spot it.
7

Word Formation Race

Goal: Master prefixes and suffixes.

Teams race to manipulate root words into nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs.

Read more
Whiteboard with a table drawn, markers.
  1. Draw a grid on the board with columns: Root, Noun, Verb, Adjective, Adverb.
  2. Write a root word in the first column (e.g., "Create", "Success", "Danger").
  3. In a relay style, teams race to fill out the rest of the row (e.g., Creation, Create, Creative, Creatively).
  4. They earn bonus points for including negative prefixes (e.g., Uncreative).
This directly trains students for the Use of English paper in Cambridge exams. Emphasize spelling changes when suffixes are added (like dropping the 'e' or changing 'y' to 'i').
8

Concept Mapping

Goal: Brainstorm thematic vocabulary.

Groups build extensive vocabulary networks around broad, abstract IELTS/TOEFL topics.

Read more
Large poster paper, markers.
  1. Provide each group with an abstract topic (e.g., "The Environment", "Technology and Privacy", "Urbanization").
  2. Groups have 5 minutes to create a mind map filled with related B2/C1 vocabulary, phrasal verbs, and collocations.
  3. They must group words logically (e.g., causes, effects, solutions).
  4. Groups display their posters and brief the class on 5 new or complex words they included.
Challenge students to avoid basic words. If their topic is "Environment", ban the words "bad", "sad", or "trash", forcing them to use "detrimental", "devastating", or "waste".

Grammar Games

Perfect mixed conditionals, advanced passives, inversion, and emphasis.

1

Inversion Race

Goal: Emphasize with negative adverbs.

Students rewrite standard sentences into emphatic inverted structures under time pressure.

Read more
Whiteboard, list of standard sentences.
  1. Write a standard sentence on the board: "I had never seen such a beautiful sunset."
  2. Teams race to the board to rewrite it using negative inversion: "Never had I seen such a beautiful sunset."
  3. Progress to harder structures involving 'Not only... but also', 'Hardly... when', or 'No sooner... than'.
Remind students that inversion behaves like a question form. The auxiliary verb MUST swap places with the subject immediately after the negative adverb.
2

Regrets & Speculations

Goal: Master mixed and 3rd conditionals.

Students read hypothetical disastrous scenarios and express regrets using advanced grammar.

Read more
Scenario cards detailing unfortunate past decisions.
  1. Distribute scenario cards (e.g., "You stayed up until 4 AM playing video games, and today you failed your final exam.").
  2. Students must write or speak sentences expressing regret using "I wish / If only + Past Perfect".
  3. They then use a Third Conditional to show the alternate reality: "If I had gone to bed earlier, I would have passed the exam."
  4. Follow up with a Mixed Conditional (past action, present result): "If I had studied, I wouldn't be crying right now."
Draw a clear timeline on the board illustrating how the 3rd conditional stays entirely in the past, while the mixed conditional links the past to the present.
3

Advanced Passive Tic-Tac-Toe

Goal: Formulate reporting passives.

Teams transform active rumors into formal passive reporting structures to claim squares.

Read more
Whiteboard drawn with a 3x3 grid.
  1. Write active rumors in the squares (e.g., "People say the company is going bankrupt").
  2. Teams must transform the sentence into a formal passive structure to claim the square.
  3. They can use "It is said that the company..." OR the more advanced "The company is said to be...".
  4. Require them to use perfect infinitives for past rumors ("The CEO is believed to have stolen...").
This structure is vital for B2/C1 essay writing (academic reporting). Be unforgiving with auxiliary verbs and infinitive forms.
4

Cleft Sentence Relay

Goal: Shift sentence focus dynamically.

Teams compete to rewrite sentences using 'It was...' or 'What...' to emphasize specific information.

Read more
Whiteboard, list of base sentences.
  1. Write a sentence: "David broke the expensive vase yesterday."
  2. Call out what needs emphasis (e.g., "Emphasize WHO did it", "Emphasize WHAT was broken").
  3. Students run to the board and write: "It was David who broke the vase" or "What David broke was the expensive vase."
  4. The first grammatically perfect sentence wins.
Cleft sentences are the secret weapon for sounding like a native speaker. Show them how this structure changes the intonation and stress when spoken aloud.
5

Modal Deduction Ball

Goal: Speculate on past events.

Students look at a mysterious image or scenario and use past modals to deduce what happened.

Read more
A soft ball, images of mysterious situations (e.g., a broken window, an abandoned car).
  1. Project a provocative image (e.g., a half-eaten cake on the floor).
  2. Throw the ball to a student. They must make a deduction using past modals (must have, might have, can't have, could have).
  3. Example: "The dog MUST HAVE eaten the cake." or "A thief COULD HAVE broken in."
  4. They throw the ball to another student who must offer a different deduction.
Write the structures explicitly on the board: [Modal + have + Past Participle]. B2 students often mistakenly say "must ate" instead of "must have eaten".
6

Advanced Error Correction

Goal: Identify subtle syntactic flaws.

Teams act as editors, diagnosing and fixing highly nuanced errors in complex academic sentences.

Read more
Worksheet containing 10 flawed B2-level sentences.
  1. Provide sentences with typical B2 errors (e.g., dangling participles: "Walking down the street, the trees were beautiful," or article misuse with uncountable nouns).
  2. In groups, students must hunt down the errors and rewrite the sentence correctly.
  3. They must be able to explain the grammatical rule they applied to fix the sentence.
  4. Award points for both the correct fix and the grammatical explanation.
Do not make the errors obvious. At B2, errors shouldn't be "He go to school," but rather "He suggested me to go to the doctor" (should be "He suggested I go / suggested going").
7

Participle Clause Story

Goal: Upgrade narrative flow.

Students reduce relative clauses and conjugate conjunctions into sleek participle clauses.

Read more
A clunky, repetitive story text.
  1. Give students a paragraph with clunky grammar: "Because he was exhausted, John went to bed. The bed, which was made of wood, was cold."
  2. Students must upgrade the text using present (-ing) or past (-ed) participle clauses.
  3. Correction: "Being exhausted, John went to bed. Made of wood, the bed was cold."
  4. Have them read their upgraded, "novelist-style" texts aloud.
Warn students about the danger of "dangling modifiers." The subject of the participle clause must be identical to the subject of the main clause.
8

Cohesion Jigsaw

Goal: Apply advanced discourse markers.

Groups reassemble a complex essay, relying heavily on advanced transition words and referential pronouns.

Read more
A B2 essay cut into single sentences or small chunks.
  1. Take an argumentative essay containing words like "Nevertheless", "Consequently", "In contrast", and "The former/The latter".
  2. Mix the sentences and have groups reconstruct the essay perfectly.
  3. Once assembled, groups must highlight the specific cohesive devices that guided their decisions.
This trains logic as much as grammar. Discuss how "Moreover" adds supporting information, while "Conversely" introduces a counter-argument.

Conversation Games

Debate ethics, negotiate business, and manage complex roleplays.

1

Ethical Dilemmas

Goal: Discuss moral gray areas.

Students are presented with difficult moral choices and must debate the best course of action.

Read more
Scenario cards detailing ethical problems (e.g., the Trolley Problem, whistleblowing at work).
  1. Present a complex dilemma: "You discover your best friend is stealing from the company you both work for. What do you do?"
  2. Put students in groups of 3-4 to debate the consequences of various actions.
  3. They must reach a consensus and present their final, unified decision to the class.
  4. The class can challenge their reasoning.
Provide language scaffolding for hypothetical thinking: "If I were in that situation, I would probably...", "The ethical thing to do would be...", "I couldn't bring myself to...".
2

Boardroom Negotiation

Goal: Reach a business consensus.

Students take on corporate roles and negotiate budgets, salaries, or project scopes.

Read more
Role cards with specific objectives, red lines, and budgets.
  1. Divide the class into pairs. One is Management, one is the Union Rep (or Buyer/Seller).
  2. Give them conflicting data: Management wants to cut costs by 10%; the Union wants a 5% raise.
  3. Set a timer for 10 minutes. They must negotiate using formal business English.
  4. If they fail to reach an agreement before time runs out, both sides "lose".
Teach them the language of compromise: "We might be able to agree to X, on the condition that you...", "That's out of the question, but we could offer...".
3

Conflict Resolution

Goal: Navigate tension with diplomacy.

Students roleplay difficult interpersonal situations, practicing tact, apologies, and assertive communication.

Read more
Scenario cards (e.g., a noisy neighbor, a group member not doing their work).
  1. Give pairs a high-tension scenario: "Your neighbor plays loud music at 2 AM every night. You need to sleep. Confront them."
  2. One plays the confrontational party; the other plays the defensive/oblivious party.
  3. They must manage the conflict without the conversation breaking down into an argument.
  4. The class observes and evaluates who handled the situation most diplomatically.
This is excellent for teaching mitigating language: "I don't mean to be unreasonable, but...", "I completely understand your perspective, however...", "Would you mind...".
4

Pitch a Startup

Goal: Deliver persuasive presentations.

Groups invent a novel business idea and aggressively pitch it to "investors" to secure fake funding.

Read more
Presentation planning sheets, fake investor money.
  1. Groups have 10 minutes to brainstorm a tech startup or revolutionary product.
  2. They must prepare a 3-minute structured pitch: The Problem, The Solution, The Market, and The Ask (money needed).
  3. They present to the rest of the class, who act as "Venture Capitalists".
  4. The VCs interrogate the business plan ("What is your revenue model?"), and then decide who to fund.
Ensure the Q&A section is robust. B2 students need to be able to defend their ideas spontaneously when challenged.
5

Devil's Advocate Debates

Goal: Argue against personal bias.

Students are forced to passionately argue the side of an issue they fundamentally disagree with.

Read more
List of controversial B2/C1 topics (e.g., Universal Basic Income, AI replacing jobs).
  1. Ask a student their opinion on a topic (e.g., "Do you think zoos are cruel?"). They answer "Yes."
  2. Tell them: "Great. Now, you have 2 minutes to convince me that zoos are the most ethical and essential institutions on Earth."
  3. The student must suppress their personal bias and construct a logical, convincing argument for the opposing side.
This removes emotional attachment and focuses entirely on the mechanics of building a logical argument in English. It's fantastic for IELTS writing task 2 prep.
6

Spontaneous Monologue

Goal: Sustain extended fluency.

Students draw a random abstract prompt and must speak continuously on the subject for two full minutes.

Read more
A hat or bowl containing abstract prompt cards (e.g., "Success", "Regret", "The Future").
  1. A student draws a prompt from the hat. They are given exactly 30 seconds to gather their thoughts.
  2. Start a timer. The student must speak about the topic, their personal experiences related to it, and their broader opinions for 2 straight minutes.
  3. If they pause for more than 5 seconds, the teacher gently prompts them with a "Wh-" question to keep them going.
Teach students how to structure a monologue (Past/Present/Future approach, or Personal/Society approach) so they don't run out of things to say after 30 seconds.
7

Crisis Management

Goal: Handle aggressive questioning.

A PR team must face the media (the class) to explain a massive corporate disaster or scandal.

Read more
A brief outlining a fictional PR disaster (e.g., an oil spill, a data breach).
  1. Assign 2-3 students as the PR team for a disgraced company. Give them 5 minutes to prepare a statement.
  2. The rest of the class acts as aggressive journalists looking for a scoop.
  3. The PR team reads their statement, then opens the floor to Q&A.
  4. Journalists try to poke holes in the statement; the PR team must defuse the situation politely but firmly.
Teach evasive or delaying tactics: "We are still looking into the details of that," "I can't comment on an ongoing investigation," or "Our primary focus right now is..."
8

Panel Discussion

Goal: Maintain an academic discourse.

Students take on the personas of different experts to discuss a global issue comprehensively.

Read more
Role cards defining expert perspectives (e.g., Economist, Environmentalist, Sociologist).
  1. Select a topic (e.g., "The impact of switching entirely to electric cars").
  2. Assign roles to 4 students. One is a car manufacturer, one an environmental scientist, one a politician, and one the moderator.
  3. The moderator guides the conversation, asking specific questions to each expert.
  4. Experts must answer in character, using field-specific vocabulary, and respectfully debate the other experts.
The moderator plays the most crucial role here. Teach them how to transition ("Let's turn to Dr. Smith for the economic perspective...") and how to interrupt politely ("If I could just stop you there...").

Reading Games

Deconstruct arguments, analyze tone, and synthesize conflicting sources.

1

Synthesizing Sources

Goal: Cross-reference information.

Students read two articles with conflicting viewpoints and must write a balanced summary identifying common ground.

Read more
Two short articles on the same topic but with different biases (e.g., one pro-nuclear energy, one anti-nuclear).
  1. Divide the class. Group A reads the "Pro" article; Group B reads the "Anti" article.
  2. Pair up one student from Group A with one from Group B.
  3. They must verbally explain their article's stance without letting their partner read it.
  4. Together, they must draft a neutral paragraph summarizing both arguments and finding one point of agreement.
This simulates university-level research. Emphasize the use of contrastive linkers like "Whereas Source A claims... Source B highlights...".
2

Author's Tone Analysis

Goal: Identify subjective framing.

Students analyze a seemingly objective text to uncover the author's hidden bias, sarcasm, or underlying tone.

Read more
An editorial, a satirical piece, or a highly opinionated review. Highlighters.
  1. Provide a text that uses strong rhetorical devices (e.g., an exaggerated review of a bad movie).
  2. Ask students: "Is the author being literal here? What is their true attitude?"
  3. Have students highlight words with positive connotations in green and negative in pink to visually map the bias.
  4. Discuss how modal verbs or specific adjectives reveal the author's true feelings.
B2 students often take English texts too literally. This exercise trains them to recognize irony and sarcasm, a key part of cultural fluency.
3

Argument Deconstruction

Goal: Map out logical structures.

Students read an opinion piece and visually diagram the premise, supporting evidence, and conclusions.

Read more
An argumentative essay (e.g., IELTS Task 2 model answer), blank paper.
  1. Provide a well-structured argumentative essay.
  2. Students must read it and draw a flowchart.
  3. They extract the "Thesis Statement" (Top), "Main Points" (Middle), "Examples/Evidence" (Branches), and "Conclusion" (Bottom).
  4. They then critically evaluate if the evidence provided actually supports the main points.
If they can deconstruct a good argument reading it, they can construct a good argument when writing it. Use this as a bridge to essay writing.
4

Paragraph Insertion

Goal: Understand macro-cohesion.

Students must insert missing paragraphs back into a long article, relying on textual context clues.

Read more
An article with paragraphs removed and placed in a randomized bank.
  1. Give students an article with empty numbered gaps where paragraphs used to be.
  2. Provide the missing paragraphs on a separate sheet, plus one extra paragraph that doesn't belong (a distractor).
  3. Students must analyze the sentence immediately before and after the gap to choose the correct paragraph.
  4. Review the pronoun referencing (e.g., "This issue...") that links the paragraphs together.
This is exactly like the FCE/CAE Reading and Use of English parts. Teach them to look for chronological clues, contrasting ideas, or pronoun antecedents to solve it.
5

Reading Between the Lines

Goal: Infer unstated information.

Students answer comprehension questions based on what is implied, rather than what is explicitly written.

Read more
A literary excerpt or a subtle email exchange.
  1. Provide a text rich in subtext. (e.g., A polite but passive-aggressive corporate email, or a character description in a novel).
  2. Ask inferential questions: "How does the boss really feel about the project?", "What can we guess about the character's financial status?".
  3. Students must point to specific words or phrasing choices that led them to their conclusion, defending their interpretation.
Focus heavily on adverbs and modifiers (e.g., "He *hastily* threw the papers in the drawer" implies panic or secrecy, not just speed).
6

Literary Scramble

Goal: Analyze narrative and stylistic flow.

Groups reorder a descriptive or literary text, relying on subtle shifts in imagery or tension.

Read more
A short story or descriptive scene cut into paragraphs.
  1. Select a text that relies on building tension or shifting focus (e.g., describing a calm morning that turns into a storm).
  2. Scramble the paragraphs and have groups reassemble them.
  3. Because it's a literary text, there may not be obvious transition words (like "firstly", "therefore").
  4. Students must rely on logic: setting the scene -> introducing the character -> rising action -> climax.
This trains students to feel the "rhythm" of English writing. Ask them to identify how the author uses short sentences to build speed or long sentences to slow the reader down.
7

Speed Reading for Bias

Goal: Evaluate credibility rapidly.

Students scan multiple short news reports to identify which ones are objective and which contain subjective framing.

Read more
3-4 short news briefs on the same event from different sources (e.g., BBC vs. a tabloid).
  1. Provide reports on the same fictional or real event.
  2. Give students exactly 3 minutes to read all of them.
  3. Ask them to rank the texts from most objective to most biased.
  4. They must quickly underline the "loaded" words (e.g., "The politician *stated*" vs "The politician *whined*") that expose the bias.
This is excellent for media literacy. Teach them that verbs like "claim," "admit," or "insist" carry heavy subtext compared to the neutral "say".
8

Open Cloze Challenge

Goal: Supply missing structural grammar.

Students fill in functional gaps in a text relying purely on their internal knowledge of grammar and collocations.

Read more
A B2-level text with functional words (prepositions, articles, auxiliaries) removed. No word bank.
  1. Provide the cloze text. Crucially, there are NO options to choose from.
  2. Students read the text and must deduce the exact word needed for the gap.
  3. Example: "She has been living here ____ 2010." (Answer: since). "He depends ____ his parents." (Answer: on).
  4. Students work individually, then compare answers with a partner to debate discrepancies before the class review.
This is the ultimate test of grammatical accuracy. If they suggest a synonym that grammatically fits, accept it, but discuss which word is more natural in the context.

All Skills Review

Simulate exams, run complex projects, and conduct peer reviews.

1

Jeopardy (B2 Edition)

Goal: High-level comprehensive review.

A competitive game focusing on idioms, complex syntax, spontaneous debate, and listening comprehension.

Read more
Interactive presentation or whiteboard grid.
  1. Create categories: Phrasal Verbs, Advanced Error Correction, 1-Minute Debates, Idiom Meanings.
  2. Assign points (100-500) based on difficulty.
  3. Teams select a category. A 500-point Grammar task might require them to rewrite an active paragraph completely in the passive voice.
  4. Teams must formulate their answers correctly to win the points.
Make the rules strict. If they give the right answer but make a basic grammar mistake while delivering it, they don't get the points. B2 requires precision.
2

Simulated Exam Stations

Goal: Replicate testing conditions.

Students rotate through rigorous, timed stations mimicking the sections of an IELTS or Cambridge exam.

Read more
Exam-style worksheets, timers, audio equipment.
  1. Set up 4 stations: Reading (True/False/Not Given), Writing (Analyze a graph), Listening (Multiple choice from a lecture), Speaking (Partner discussion task).
  2. Impose strict exam conditions (no talking unless at the Speaking station).
  3. Give students 12 minutes per station. Ring a bell to enforce rotation.
  4. Review the strategies and answers as a class afterward.
This is less about "fun" and more about stamina and time management. It demystifies the exam process and reduces test anxiety.
3

The UN Summit

Goal: Formal debate and drafting.

Students represent countries, research policies, debate global issues, and draft formal resolutions.

Read more
Country profiles, topic briefs (e.g., Climate Change, Trade), paper.
  1. Assign pairs a country. Give them 10 minutes to review their country's stance on the issue (Reading).
  2. Open the floor for a moderated debate (Speaking/Listening). Countries must state their positions and form alliances.
  3. Alliances must draft a short, formal treaty or resolution to solve the problem (Writing).
  4. Vote on the best resolution.
Provide a list of diplomatic vocabulary: "We urge the committee to...", "It is our firm belief that...", "We strongly oppose...".
4

Marketing Campaign

Goal: Write across different registers.

Groups design a product and create a multi-media campaign involving press releases, slogans, and pitches.

Read more
Large paper, markers.
  1. Groups invent a new product or service.
  2. They must write a formal press release introducing it (Writing - Formal).
  3. They design a poster with a catchy, idiomatic slogan (Vocabulary - Informal).
  4. They must perform a 30-second persuasive radio ad for the class (Speaking - Persuasive).
  5. The class votes on the most convincing campaign.
This highlights how English changes based on the audience. The formal press release uses passive voice; the radio ad uses imperatives and direct address.
5

Murder Mystery

Goal: Synthesize data to solve a problem.

Students read witness testimonies, interrogate suspects, and write a formal police report.

Read more
Character profiles, clues, witness statements.
  1. Hand out background reading materials outlining the crime scene and suspects (Reading).
  2. Assign a few students to be the suspects. The rest are detectives who must interview them to find inconsistencies (Speaking/Listening).
  3. After gathering all data, detectives must write a concluding report identifying the murderer and detailing their motive and method (Writing).
This naturally elicits past perfect ("He had already left when...") and modals of deduction ("She must have been lying because...").
6

The Assessment Center

Goal: Demonstrate collaborative leadership.

A group interview simulation where students must solve a logic problem together while being evaluated.

Read more
A complex logic puzzle or survival scenario (e.g., stranded on an island, pick 5 items).
  1. Put 4-5 students in a group in the center of the room. They are "job applicants."
  2. Give them a difficult scenario to solve collaboratively within 10 minutes.
  3. The rest of the class are "HR Managers" observing silently and taking notes on their English fluency, ability to include others, and persuasion skills.
  4. HR provides feedback on who gets the job and why.
Teach applicants language for managing discussions: "That's a valid point, but we should also consider...", "John, what are your thoughts on this?".
7

Collaborative Essay

Goal: Structure academic arguments.

Groups co-author a standard 4-paragraph academic essay, focusing heavily on linking devices and structure.

Read more
Large poster paper, markers, an essay prompt.
  1. Give groups an essay prompt (e.g., "The advantages and disadvantages of remote work").
  2. Student 1 writes the Introduction (Thesis). They pass the paper.
  3. Student 2 writes Body Paragraph 1 (Advantages). Student 3 writes Body Paragraph 2 (Disadvantages).
  4. Student 4 writes the Conclusion.
  5. The group reviews the whole text to ensure it flows logically and cohesively.
Demand the use of high-level discourse markers: "Proponents argue that...", "Conversely...", "To summarize...". The paragraphs must connect smoothly.
8

Peer Review Workshop

Goal: Evaluate and upgrade texts.

Students swap their written work and act as editors, actively upgrading vocabulary and fixing grammar.

Read more
Students' own written homework, red/green pens, a correction rubric.
  1. Students bring a previously written essay or report to class.
  2. They swap papers with a partner. Provide a strict rubric: 1. Check tenses, 2. Check articles, 3. Upgrade 5 basic words to advanced B2 words.
  3. Editors use a red pen for grammar fixes and a green pen for vocabulary upgrades (e.g., changing "bad" to "detrimental").
  4. Partners discuss the edits and justify their suggestions.
B2 students learn incredibly fast by analyzing their peers' mistakes. It shifts them from passive learners to active analytical thinkers.

Listening Games

Comprehend native speed, infer attitudes, and take academic notes.

1

Podcast Note-taking

Goal: Filter and record information.

Students listen to an authentic English podcast and practice taking structured Cornell notes.

Read more
A 3-minute clip of an engaging podcast (e.g., TED Radio Hour), paper.
  1. Teach students a note-taking system (e.g., symbols, abbreviations, indenting).
  2. Play the podcast clip straight through once. Students take rapid notes.
  3. Play it a second time for them to fill in missing details.
  4. In pairs, they use ONLY their notes to reconstruct the speaker's main argument.
Do not give them specific questions beforehand. At B2, they need to independently identify what information is important versus what is filler.
2

Fact-checking the News

Goal: Compare audio to written data.

Students read a written summary, then listen to the live broadcast and identify factual discrepancies.

Read more
A written news summary, audio of the actual broadcast.
  1. Provide a short, written summary of a news event. Tell students there are 5 errors in the text.
  2. Play the authentic news broadcast audio.
  3. Students must listen intensively and cross out the incorrect information on their paper, writing in the correct facts based on the audio.
  4. Review the corrections together.
Make the errors subtle. Instead of changing a name, change a nuance (e.g., the text says "The policy was rejected," but the audio says "The policy was postponed").

Idiom Context Catch

Goal: Deduce figurative meaning.

Students listen to dialogues containing idioms and must infer their meaning from the speaker's tone and context.

Read more
Audio clips of native speakers using idioms in conversation.
  1. Play a short audio dialogue containing an idiom (e.g., "I tried to explain the software to him, but it went completely *over his head*.").
  2. Pause the audio. Ask students to write down what they think the idiom means based purely on the situation.
  3. Have them discuss their theories in pairs before revealing the standard definition.
Remind students not to panic when they hear words they don't know. The surrounding context almost always provides enough clues to guess the general sentiment.

Consecutive Interpreting

Goal: Retain and paraphrase chunks.

Students listen to long chunks of speech, take notes, and summarize it back to their partner.

Read more
A complex article to read aloud.
  1. Pair students. Partner A puts their pen down. Partner B gets ready to take notes.
  2. Read a 45-second chunk of an article at a natural speed.
  3. Partner B uses their notes to explain the entire chunk to Partner A in their own words.
  4. Partner A asks clarifying questions. Swap roles for the next chunk.
This prevents word-for-word translation. They are forced to listen for the underlying meaning and reconstruct it syntactically.

Lecture Gap-fill

Goal: Follow academic structures.

Students listen to an academic lecture and fill out a complex outline, catching main points and sub-points.

Read more
An academic lecture audio, a skeletal outline worksheet.
  1. Provide a worksheet structured like an academic outline with Roman numerals and bullet points, leaving key sections blank.
  2. Play a 5-minute lecture (e.g., on History or Biology).
  3. Students listen for signpost words ("There are three main reasons...", "Turning now to...") to navigate the outline and fill in the blanks.
Teach students to listen specifically for discourse markers. When a speaker says "On the other hand," students should know they need to jump to the contrasting section of their notes.

Attitude Detection

Goal: Analyze intonation and stress.

Students listen to ambiguous statements and determine the speaker's mood (sarcastic, angry, hesitant) based on tone.

Read more
Audio clips of speakers showing varying emotions, or the teacher acting them out.
  1. Play an audio clip of a phrase like "Oh, that's just great."
  2. Ask the students: "Is the speaker genuinely happy, or are they being sarcastic?"
  3. Have them identify the vocal cues (e.g., falling intonation, heavy sigh) that led to their conclusion.
  4. Replay the phrase with a different intonation to contrast the meanings.
This is crucial for real-world interaction. English relies heavily on word stress to change meaning ("*I* didn't say that" vs "I didn't say *that*").

TED Talk Transcription

Goal: Decode fast connected speech.

Students repeatedly listen to a short, fast clip of a TED talk and attempt to transcribe it word-for-word.

Read more
A 20-30 second video clip of a TED talk, paper.
  1. Choose a short segment where the speaker talks quickly or uses complex grammar.
  2. Play the clip 3 or 4 times. Students attempt to write down every single word.
  3. Have them compare their transcriptions with a partner to fill in gaps.
  4. Reveal the actual transcript on the board. Discuss why certain words (like "have" or "to") were hard to hear due to linking and reduction.
Explain "weak forms" (e.g., how "going to" sounds like "gonna", or how "would have" sounds like "woulda"). Recognizing this unlocks native-level listening.

Debate Mapping

Goal: Track complex arguments.

Students listen to a heated debate between two people and must map out who made which argument and the counter-arguments used.

Read more
Audio/Video of a panel discussion or news debate.
  1. Play a 3-minute clip of two people arguing a topic (e.g., a TV news debate).
  2. Divide a paper into two columns (Speaker A and Speaker B).
  3. Students listen and jot down the core argument of Speaker A, and exactly how Speaker B attempts to refute it.
  4. Discuss the flow of the argument afterward. Who won? Why?
This trains high-level academic listening. Students must learn to separate the speaker's emotional delivery from the actual logical points being made.
